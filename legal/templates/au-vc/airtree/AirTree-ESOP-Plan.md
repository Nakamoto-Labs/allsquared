**Schedule 1**

# **How does an Employee Share Option Plan (ESOP) operate?**

> Option plans are often used to retain key talent and to provide a longer term incentive to stay with the company.
>
> Where employees receive options under an ESOP, they receive a right (but not an obligation) to purchase shares in the company at a specified time in the future, for an agreed price (the exercise price). When employees convert their options into shares this is referred to as exercising their options. Generally, options cannot be exercised immediately but might require certain conditions to be satisfied before they can be exercised. These are often referred to as vesting conditions and typically relate to company performance and/or or length of service.
>
> Options usually have an exercise period of three to four years. During the period that an employee holds the options they are generally not entitled to dividends or voting rights, and generally cannot sell their options. However, once options are exercised and the underlying shares issued, employees generally receive the full benefits of being a shareholder.

# **Vesting criteria**

### The invitation to eligible employees may include details of any vesting criteria before the options acquired by the employee become fully vested and are capable of exercise. These criteria may include minimum tenure periods specified by the Board at the time of invitation.

### Once any vesting criteria are satisfied, the options become capable of exercise, subject to the participant paying the exercise price to the company. 

# **Rights attaching to shares issued under the ESOP**

### Shares issued under an ESOP must be ordinary shares.

# **Leavers**

# Usually, all unvested options automatically lapse with effect on the date the Optionholder ceases to be employed. Vested but unexercised options may also be subject to similar arrangements, but it is not uncommon for the relevant employee to have an ability to exercise the option within a certain period of time following cessation of employment. \"Good leaver\"/\"bad leaver\" arrangements often dictate what happens at this point in time.

# **Buy-back rights**

### The company may have the right to buy-back shares acquired on exercise of options. This is particularly the case if the employee is a "bad leaver". If the company may buy-back shares from "good leavers" and "bad leavers", the prices at which those shares may be bought back will generally differ depending on whether the employee is a "good leaver" or a "bad leaver".

# **Concessional tax rules under 'ESS start-up concession'** 

> Options (issued under a Start-up compliant ESOP) will not be taxed on grant, vesting or exercise. Rather, the taxing point will be deferred until the sale of the shares (or disposal of the options) which will generally be a CGT event. The 50% CGT discount will apply if the sale of the shares occurs at least 12 months after the grant of the Options.

# **What are the 'start-up eligibility criteria'?** 

## The company that grants the ESS interests (options in this case) must have had an aggregated turnover of less than \$50 million in the income year prior to the year the interests are granted. The turnover test includes connected entities but certain eligible investors in the company, including eligible venture capital investors and any exempt entity that is a deductable gift recipient, are treated as unconnected. 

## The company (and its corporate group) must not have any interests listed on an approved stock exchange in the income year prior to the ESS interest being offered. 

### The company (and its connected entities) must have been incorporated for less than 10 years. 

### The employing company (which can be a subsidiary of the company granting the ESS interests are being offered) must be an Australian resident taxpayer. 

# **Eligibility criteria for the terms of the ESS offer** 

## The only interests that can be acquired under the ESS must be ordinary shares or options to acquire ordinary shares, or rights (including Options) to acquire ordinary shares. 

## The ESS interests must be held for a minimum holding period of 3 years (commencing on the date the ESS interests were acquired). However, this period can be shortened depending on circumstances.

## The exercise price must not be less than the market value of shares in the company at the date of grant of the options. 

# **Eligibility criteria for employees** 

> Employees cannot hold more than 10% ownership or voting rights in the company in which the ESS interests are being granted --- including vested and unvested options and aggregating both the current and previous grants.

# **How does a Loan Plan operate?**

## Broadly speaking, under a Loan Plan:

## the employing Company grants a loan to the employee, the proceeds of which are used to acquire (usually by way of subscription) shares in the Company; and

## in ordinary circumstances, the shares are acquired at market value.

# **What are the characteristics of the loan?**

## The loan usually:

### is limited-recourse (i.e. the company has no claim against the employee other than as against the shares issued to the employee); and

### has a benchmark rate of interest (although, whether a rate must apply may depend on a number of factors).

## The loan is repaid is generally by applying any dividends that are paid by the Company towards paying down the loan.

## Any outstanding part of the loan is usually repayable if:

### the employee ceases to be employed by the Company; or

### an exit event occurs in respect of the Company. 

# **Vesting criteria**

### The invitation to eligible employees may include details of any vesting criteria before the shares acquired by the employee become fully vested. These criteria may include minimum tenure periods specified by the Board at the time of invitation.

# **Rights attaching to shares issued under the Loan Plan**

### Shares issued under a Loan Plan may have all rights which the holder of ordinary shares has, or they may have limited rights.

# **Buy-back rights**

## As employees will receive all shares upfront, it is important to have clear methods in place for the company to buy-back (or otherwise procure the transfer of) shares if the employee ceases to be employed. Options include:

## the company has an explicit right to buy back a decreasing number of shares each month over a set period (usually 2-3 years) if the employee ceases to be employed by the company -- that is:

### on month one, the company may buy back 100% of the shares if the employee ceases to be employed during that month; 

### on month two, the company may buy back 35/36ths of the shares (based on a three year vesting cycle) if the employee ceases to be employed during that month; and

### on month three, the company may buy back 34/36ths of the shares (based on a three year vesting cycle) if the employee ceases to be employed during that month, etc.;

## the company has an explicit right to buy back a decreasing number of shares each month over a set period (usually 2-3 years), but there is a one year cliff, if the employee ceases to be employed by the company -- that is:

### at any time during the first 12 months after issue, the company may buy back 100% of the shares if the employee ceases to be employed during that 12 months; 

### on month thirteen, the company may buy back 23/36ths of the shares (based on a three year vesting cycle) if the employee ceases to be employed during that month; and

### on month fourteen, the company may buy back 22/36ths of the shares (based on a three year vesting cycle) if the employee ceases to be employed during that month, etc.;

## the company has an explicit right to buy back the shares if the employee ceases to be employed at any time during a set period; and

## the company has an explicit right to buy back the shares if the employee/company fails to reach relevant performance hurdles. 

### The buyback price where shares have not vested is usually an amount equal to the issue price per share (which is then used to repay that loan).

### In addition, the company may have the right to buy-back shares that have vested. This is particularly the case if the employee is a "bad leaver". If the company may buy-back shares from "good leavers" and "bad leavers", the prices at which those shares may be bought back will generally differ depending on whether the employee is a "good leaver" or a "bad leaver".

# **Tax consequences**

### Generally speaking, the following tax consequences will apply for participants under a loan plan:

## no income tax will be payable upfront (assuming the shares are issued at market value);

## when the shares are sold, tax will be payable on any capital gain (being the amount equal to the sale price minus the issue price). 

## Shares issued under a loan plan are immediately held on capital account and the time period to qualify for the 50% capital gains tax relief starts to run from day 1.

# **Key considerations**

## The key issues to consider with loan plans are: 

### whether any part of the loan will be deemed to be a dividend under Division 7A; and

### whether there are any fringe benefits tax issues.

## Company accounting issues need to be considered.
